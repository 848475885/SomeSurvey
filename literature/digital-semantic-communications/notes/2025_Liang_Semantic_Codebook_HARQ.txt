Semantic Codebook-Based HARQ for

Gaohong Liang , Xuefei Zhang , Senior Member, IEEE, Ji Zhang , Yao Sun , Senior Member, IEEE,
Qimei Cui , Senior Member, IEEE, and Xiaofeng Tao , Senior Member, IEEE
AbstractвЂ”Semantic communication (SemCom) lies in the
emphasis on ensuring the correct semantic understanding rather
than error-free bit transmission. However, traditional hybrid
automatic repeat request (HARQ) mechanism relies on a bit-level
check, and it cannot effectively address errors at the semantic
level. In this paper, we propose a semantic codebook-based
HARQ (SCB-HARQ) mechanism for the reliable and efficient
SemCom. To enable semantic-level error detection, SCB-HARQ
leverages a shared semantic codebook trained offline at both
the transmitter and receiver. This codebook serves as prior
information for evaluating the distortion of received semantic
features, quantifying the extent to which they deviate from
the intended meaning. To reduce the transmission overhead of
features in the codebook, a weighted semantic feature index
(WSFI) clustering method is introduced to map features into a
compact index representation. Then, a masked rate-adaptive joint
source-channel coding (JSCC) method is proposed to locate and
retransmit the distorted features. The simulation results demonstrate that the proposed SCB-HARQ outperforms the traditional
HARQ mechanism, achieving a 46.29% improvement in image
reconstruction performance while reducing the transmission data
volume by 51.27%.

feature index.

The booming development of artificial intelligence (AI) has
significantly enhanced the performance of feature extraction
and reconstruction across diverse modalities of source data,
including text [5], speech [6], images [7], [8], [9], [10], [11],
[12], [13] and videos [14]. This advancement has expedited
the evolution of communication from a syntactic to a semantic paradigm, which conveys the вЂњmeaningsвЂќ of transmitted
information rather than the raw data.
Most SemCom systems implement a deep joint sourcechannel coding (DJSCC) scheme, which typically comprises
two neural networks, as the semantic encoder and the semantic
decoder, respectively. A non-trainable layer is incorporated
between them to represent the channel. As such, the semantics of the source information is extracted, minimizing the
volume of data transmitted. Some early studies on SemCom
have shown the robustness in low signal-to-noise ratio (SNR)
and limited bandwidth conditions, compared with traditional
syntactic communication. Additionally, literature [15], [16],
[17], [18] integrate a channel adaptive module into the DJSCC
framework, further enhancing the robustness of SemCom
systems.
Despite the benefits mentioned above, it is possible that
the transmitted semantic features still become distorted due
to channel fading or interference during wireless transmission, ultimately leading to transmission failures. Thus, hybrid
automatic repeat request (HARQ) mechanism is a necessity to
improve transmission reliability. Traditional HARQ serves as
a cornerstone of reliable transmission in wireless communication systems [19]. The primary function of this mechanism
is to correct erroneous data packets. Upon detecting errors
in the received data, the receiver temporarily stores the corrupted packets and requests retransmission. The most common
technique used for error detection is cyclic redundancy check
(CRC), which depends on adding redundant bits in the original
message and then checking the received data.
In SemCom systems, the emphasis should be shifted to
the semantic-level check. As such, the conventional CRC
error detection method cannot be applied. Literature [20], [21]
utilize a semantic similarity detection network called SimCRC
for semantic error detection. Furthermore, the decoder proposed in [22] consolidates information received from previous
transmissions rather than discarding it to improve the transmission reliability. Notably, all the above-mentioned approaches
assume that the received semantic features should be totally
decoded to source data first, followed by entering SimCRC.

As a result, although these approaches achieve semantic
error detection, they introduce additional latency before
retransmission compared to the conventional CRC method,
which performs error detection prior to source decoding.
Furthermore, only a few works have explored HARQ mechanisms for SemCom, which enable semantic error detection
prior to full data decoding [23], [24]. However, these
approaches suffer from significant limitations. The approach in
[23] relies heavily on a pre-trained distortion evaluation model
for semantic error detection, making it sensitive to minor
changes and often requiring retraining without substantial performance improvements. Additionally, literature [24] utilizes
similarity ranking to detect semantic errors, but it introduces
excessive communication overhead to establish these rankings
between the transmitter and receiver, rendering it impractical
for resource-constrained scenarios. Therefore, it is imperative
to find a new retransmission mechanism for SemCom.
Currently, several studies have also proposed codebookbased SemCom frameworks to enhance robustness and
transmission efficiency. For instance, the authors in [25]
propose a vector quantized variational autoencoder (VQ-VAE)based SemCom framework, where only the indices of semantic
features within a codebook are transmitted. At the receiver,
the corresponding features are retrieved from the codebook
using the received indices, enabling the reconstruction of the
source data. Building upon this concept, [26], [27] further
employ generative models to reconstruct source content from
the received indices, thereby enhancing transmission performance by mitigating the effects of channel noise on semantic
representations. In addition, [28] utilizes a semantic-aware
codebook to improve the efficiency of semantic encoding.
Motivated by these advancements, we propose leveraging the features in the codebook as prior information for
semantic-level error checking, rather than relying on them
exclusively for data reconstruction. Specifically, by evaluating
the semantic similarity between the received features and the
corresponding features in the codebook, our method can detect
distorted features and trigger retransmission, thereby enabling
more reliable data transmission.
Therefore, this paper proposes a novel semantic codebookbased hybrid automatic retransmission request (SCB-HARQ)
mechanism that achieves semantic-level error detection. To
the best of our knowledge, this is the first work to formally introduce a codebook-guided HARQ mechanism that
enables pre-decoding retransmission decisions, thereby bridging semantic understanding and transmission reliability in a
unified framework. The main contributions of this paper are
as follows:
вЂў Semantic codebook: We design a two-stage semantic
codebook that serves as a lightweight prior for semantic error detection with minimal transmission overhead.
The codebook comprises two components: Basic Feature
Set: High-fidelity semantic feature vectors are extracted
via a VQGAN model to serve as reference for evaluating semantic consistency. Index-Map Set: To further
reduce transmission cost, we cluster the spatial distribution of features into compact index-map set using a
weighted semantic feature index-map (WSFI) metric that
accounts for both feature similarity and spatial seman-

tics. The two-stage quantization encodes features into a
compact index, enabling low-overhead transmission and
supporting semantic-level error detection at the receiver.
вЂў SCB-HARQ mechanism: The SCB-HARQ aims to
address two fundamental problems in SemCom. First,
it leverages the semantic codebook as prior information
to decide whether a semantic retransmission is needed.
Then, an masked rate-adaptive JSCC method is proposed

In this way, SCB-HARQ retransmits only the distorted

II. R ELATED W ORKS
This section reviews the existing literature on SemCom
for wireless image transmission, followed by an analysis of
HARQ mechanisms currently applied in this field, which are
designed to further enhance the reliability and efficiency of
the data transmission.
A. SemCom for Image Transmission
In wireless image transmission, the SemCom approach
involves extracting rich semantic features from images at the
transmitter. These features are then transmitted by the DJSCC
scheme, followed by reconstruction at the receiver. Inspiringly,
SemCom achieves higher image reconstruction performance
than traditional communication, especially in the low bandwidth and low SNR condition. Specifically, a DJSCC method
for wireless image transmission proposed in [7] effectively
overcomes abrupt quality degradations, known as the вЂњcliff
effectвЂќ. Afterwards, several modifications and extensions have
been provided in [15], [16], [17], [18], [29], [30], and [31]. For
instance, an adaptive SNR decoder based on DJSCC is devised
to enhance the robustness against channel noise in [15].
Furthermore, [18] integrates orthogonal frequency division
multiplexing (OFDM) into the DJSCC framework, increasing
robustness to dynamic channel conditions. Additionally, a
channel-adaptive DJSCC scheme utilizes estimated channel
state information (CSI) to allocate transmission power across
subchannels in [16]. On this basis, [17] proposes a policy
network that dynamically adjust the transmission rate based
on both the CSI and the image content, thereby promoting the
channel bandwidth utilization under the varying conditions.

Although the studies mentioned above have proved that

B. HARQ for SemCom System
HARQ stands for the hybrid automatic repeat request
(ARQ), implying the combination of forward error correction
(FEC) and ARQ feedback mechanism where the receiver sends
a retransmission request if an error is detected in the received
data. After receiving the request, the transmitter retransmits
the packets, thereby improving the transmission reliability. In
[9], the feedback mechanism is first introduced in SemCom by
providing the feedback relying on the CSI. This idea is further
extended in [10], where the encoder leverages the semantic
information and the feedback to generate coded symbols.
These studies discussed above primarily rely on the feedback from previous transmission to guide the encoding of
subsequent data, demonstrating that incorporating the positive
effect of feedback mechanisms on SemCom. Additionally,
[20] replaces the conventional CRC with a SimCRC model
to trigger retransmission. Then, [21] enhances the accuracy of
the error detection accuracy by analyzing the feature structure
of the reconstructed image. On the basis of SimCRC, [22]
proposes an incremental knowledge-based HARQ mechanism
for SemCom, where the semantic decoder integrates the
incremental knowledge from previous transmissions into the
semantic information in the retransmission.
However, the retransmission methods mentioned above need
the receiver decodes all semantic features prior to error detection, thereby resulting in the additional computing cost and
time. Furthermore, these methods usually rely on transmitting
redundant information multiple times, lacking of the ability to
accurately identify and correct the specific distorted semantic
features.
To address above problem, [23] proposed a feature distortion
evaluation (FDE) network to measure semantic similarity
between the original feature and its reconstructed counterpart before data reconstruction. However, the FDE modelвЂ™s

reliance on a predefined semantic similarity metric makes
it highly sensitive to changes in the metric formulation. As
even minor modifications to the metric function can disrupt
the metricвЂ™s distribution, often requiring retraining without
yielding substantial benefits. To overcome this limitation, [24]
utilizes similarity ranking to capture the relative semantic
relationships between original and reconstructed features. But
it is impractical in the resource-constrained scenarios due
to the cost of more exchanges between the transmitter and
receiver to establish the similarity ranking.
These limitations underscore the need for a semantic error
detection and retransmission mechanism that is both robust
to metric variations and efficient in communication overhead.
Motivated by this, we propose a semantic codebook-based
HARQ (SCB-HARQ) framework, which leverages compact
semantic priors to enable low-cost and accurate semantic-level
error control.

IV. K EY C OMPONENTS IN SCB-HARQ
This section presents a detailed explanation of some key
components in SCB-HARQ, e.g., the semantic codebook, the
masked rate-adaptive JSCC scheme and the enhanced semantic
decoder. Firstly, the semantic codebook is investigated as
a prior information for semantic error detection. Then, the
masked rate-adaptive JSCC scheme is designed to identify
which semantic features need to be retransmitted. Finally,
the enhanced semantic decoder is designed to use the quantized features and the received features together for image
reconstruction.

A. Semantic Codebook
The semantic codebook consists of two main components:
a basic feature set Z and an index-map set I. The basic
feature set Z comprises a collection of representative semantic
feature vectors extracted from image datasets. The index-map
set I, on the other hand, contains a set of prototypical spatial
configurations of feature indices, obtained by clustering the
index-maps derived from Z. During transmission, each source
image is matched to its closest index-map in I, and only
the corresponding compact index V is transmitted, instead
of the full index-map. This design substantially reduces the
transmission overhead while preserving essential semantic
structure.

we utilize the framework of VQGAN [34]. In this framework,
the semantic encoder first maps the image features to a series
of discrete feature vectors, which captures the segments of the
real texture information from the source image. Subsequently,
the semantic decoder weights and combines discrete feature
vectors to reconstruct the image.

each spatial location requires log2 (N1 ) bits. The index-map
contains M 2 entries, resulting in a total of M 2 log2 (N1 ) bits
per image. Such overhead significantly exceeds the payload
capacity of the control channel in practical wireless systems
for free error, rendering direct transmission infeasible.
To reduce transmission overhead, we employ an indexmap to convert Zq into a compact representation. This is
accomplished by clustering similar index-maps to form a
representative set, denoted as I. To this end, we introduce
a novel distance metric, termed the weighted semantic feature index-map (WSFI) distance, as detailed in Section V-B.
WSFI captures structural similarities among image indexmaps, enabling more effective clustering into a compact set
of representative patterns. Rather than transmitting the entire
index-map, only the cluster index V is sent, requiring just
log2 (N2 ) bits, where N2 is the size of I. This significantly
reduces control channel usage from M 2 log2 (N1 ) to log2 (N2 ),
while preserving the global semantic structure necessary for
robust error detection.
Overall, the semantic codebook provides auxiliary information specifically for semantic error detection, which is crucial

for balancing semantic fidelity and transmission efficiency.
The basic feature set Z ensures diverse and robust feature
representations, while the index-map set I compresses these
representations into compact indices, reducing transmission
overhead with minimal loss of semantic content.

In addition, when there are still features in the (r в€’ 1)-th
transmission that are not received correctly, a binary feedback
vector fr is generated to precisely identify features that have
undergone semantic distortion and require retransmission. It
acts as a mask to trigger the r-th retransmission. On this
basis, the distorted features, represented as Zr , are selected and
encoded by a rate-adaptive JSC encoder to produce the retransmission signal Yr . The masking mechanism ensures that only
distorted features are retransmitted, effectively minimizing
bandwidth usage. Moreover, the proposed system achieves an
optimal balance between transmission efficiency and semantic
accuracy by dynamically adjusting the encoding rate during
retransmission.
After the r-th retransmission, the receiver obtains ZМ‚r , and
S is recalculated by DEC(В·). The retransmission repeats until
S reaches a threshold or the maximum number of retransmissions is reached. Finally, ZМ‚, which aggregates features
from multiple transmissions, are combined with the quantized
features Zq to reconstruct the image.

JSC encoder-decoder pairs for the rate-adaptive JSCC scheme.
Finally, Phase 3 is integrated with the previously trained components to enable end-to-end SCB-HARQ communication.
